Thanksgiving in America may be a tradition dating back to the 17th century, but in Germany and the rest of Europe, harvest celebrations have been a fixture since pagan times. We take a look at the Erntedank harvest festival and explain why it’s sometimes considered the “Thanksgiving of Germany”. The meaning of the German Thanksgiving (Erntedank), how it differs from Thanksgiving in North America and why it is still very important for us today! On the Harvest Festival, Germany comes alive with a variety of Thanksgiving parades. These parades feature floats decked out with flowers and motifs that resemble situations related to the harvest. In addition, participants carry a large crown designed for the parade, signifying gratitude. German Thanksgiving celebrations include religious ceremonies, communal gatherings and, of course, food. The celebrations vary by region but are mostly held in rural areas. Often, there’s a parade with beautifully decorated floats and people in traditional clothing, creating the atmosphere of a country fair. From rustic centerpieces to colorful wreaths, Thanksgiving decor in Germany is sure to impress. One of the most popular Thanksgiving decorations in Germany is the cornucopia. Also known as a “horn of plenty,” this decorative object is typically filled with seasonal fruits and vegetables.
